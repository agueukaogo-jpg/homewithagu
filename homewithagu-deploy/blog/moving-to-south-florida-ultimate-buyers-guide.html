Moving to South Florida: The Ultimate Buyer's Guide

May 14, 2026 · 10 min read

South Florida continues to be one of the top relocation destinations in the country. Between the year-round warm weather, zero state income tax, world-class dining and entertainment, and a real estate market that rewards smart buyers, it is no surprise that thousands of families and professionals make the move every year.

But relocating to South Florida is different from visiting on vacation. The housing market moves fast, insurance costs can catch newcomers off guard, and the difference between neighborhoods can be dramatic — even within the same zip code. This guide covers everything you need to know to make a confident, well-informed purchase in Miami-Dade, Broward, or Palm Beach County.

The Financial Picture: What to Expect

No State Income Tax

Florida has no state income tax, which is one of the biggest financial draws for people relocating from states like New York, California, or New Jersey. Depending on your income level, this can represent tens of thousands of dollars in annual savings — money that many buyers put directly toward their mortgage or investment properties.

Property Taxes

While there is no income tax, Florida does collect property taxes. The effective rate in Miami-Dade County typically runs between 1.8% and 2.2% of your home's assessed value. Broward County is similar, while Palm Beach County tends to be slightly lower. If you are purchasing a primary residence, you can claim a Homestead Exemption that reduces your taxable value by up to $50,000 — a meaningful savings that kicks in the year after you establish residency.

Insurance Costs

This is where many newcomers get surprised. South Florida has some of the highest property insurance premiums in the nation due to hurricane risk. Expect to pay anywhere from $3,000 to $10,000 or more annually depending on the property type, location, age of the roof, and proximity to the coast. Flood insurance is often required as well, particularly in zones near the water. Always get insurance quotes before making an offer so you understand your true monthly cost.

Pro Tip: Calculate Your True Monthly Cost

Before you fall in love with a home, add up: mortgage payment + property taxes + HOA fees + homeowner's insurance + flood insurance. In South Florida, the non-mortgage costs can easily add $1,500 to $3,000 per month depending on the property.

Choosing the Right Area

Miami-Dade County

Miami-Dade is the cultural and economic engine of South Florida. It offers the most diverse range of housing — from ultra-luxury waterfront penthouses in Brickell and Miami Beach to family-friendly suburban communities in Doral, Kendall, and Homestead. If you want nightlife, international culture, and career opportunities in finance, tech, or hospitality, Miami-Dade is the center of gravity.

Broward County (Fort Lauderdale)

Broward offers a slightly more relaxed pace than Miami while still providing excellent beaches, dining, and employment. Fort Lauderdale's Las Olas Boulevard is one of the most desirable addresses in South Florida, and neighborhoods like Wilton Manors, Coral Ridge, and Plantation offer strong value for families. Broward is also home to Port Everglades and Fort Lauderdale-Hollywood International Airport, making it convenient for travelers.

Palm Beach County

Palm Beach County attracts retirees, families seeking top-rated schools, and buyers who want more space for their money. Cities like Boca Raton, Delray Beach, and Jupiter offer a polished suburban lifestyle with excellent amenities. The market here tends to be slightly less volatile than Miami, making it appealing for buyers who prioritize stability.

The Home Buying Process in Florida

Step 1: Get Pre-Approved. Before you start looking at homes, get a mortgage pre-approval from a lender familiar with the Florida market. This tells sellers you are a serious buyer and gives you a clear budget. If you are relocating from another state, make sure your lender understands Florida-specific requirements.

Step 2: Hire a Local Agent. South Florida is a market of micro-neighborhoods. An agent who lives and works here daily can identify opportunities, flag potential issues with specific buildings or HOAs, and negotiate effectively on your behalf. Look for someone with deep local market knowledge and a track record of representing buyers — not just listings.

Step 3: Tour and Make an Offer. In a competitive market, you may need to act quickly when you find the right property. Your agent will help you structure a compelling offer that balances price, terms, and contingencies. In South Florida, cash offers are common, but well-structured financed offers absolutely win deals when presented correctly.

Step 4: Inspection and Due Diligence. Always get a full home inspection, even on new construction. In South Florida, pay particular attention to the roof condition, hurricane impact windows, plumbing (particularly in older homes), and any signs of water intrusion. For condos, review the building's reserve study and financial statements carefully — recent Florida legislation has tightened requirements for condo associations, and some buildings face significant special assessments.

Step 5: Closing. Florida closings typically take 30 to 45 days for financed purchases and can be as quick as two weeks for cash. Title insurance is standard and protects you against any liens or ownership disputes. Your agent and closing attorney will walk you through every document.

Lifestyle Considerations

Weather: South Florida is warm and humid from May through October, with afternoon thunderstorms almost daily in summer. Hurricane season runs from June through November. Most residents adapt quickly, and modern construction is built to withstand severe weather.

Transportation: South Florida is a car-dependent region. While the Brightline train now connects Miami, Fort Lauderdale, and West Palm Beach (and extends to Orlando), daily life generally requires a vehicle. Factor in commute times when choosing your neighborhood — traffic on I-95 and the Turnpike can be significant during peak hours.

Schools: If you have children, school quality varies widely by district and even by neighborhood. Broward and Palm Beach counties generally have higher-rated public schools than Miami-Dade, though Miami has excellent charter and private school options. Always research school zones before purchasing.

Culture and Recreation: This is where South Florida truly shines. Between the beaches, Everglades National Park, Art Basel, the Design District, world-class restaurants, professional sports teams, and a thriving nightlife scene, there is something for every lifestyle. The diversity of the region — with strong Latin American, Caribbean, and European influences — makes it one of the most culturally rich areas in the United States.
